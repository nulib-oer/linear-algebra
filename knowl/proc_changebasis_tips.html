Procedure 5.3.19. Change of basis computational tips.

Let \(B=(\boldv_1, \boldv_2, \dots, \boldv_n)\) and \(B'\) be ordered bases of the vector space \(V\text{.}\) Below you find a variety of techniques for computing \(\underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P}\) and \(\underset{B'\rightarrow B}{P}\text{.}\)
  1. To compute \(\underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P}\) directly, we must compute \([\boldv_j]_{B'}\) for each \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\) This typically involves setting up and solving a linear system.
  2. We have \(\underset{B'\rightarrow B}{P}=(\underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P})^{-1}\text{.}\) This observation is useful in situations where (a) one change of basis matrix is easier to compute than the other and (b) computing inverse matrices is not too onerous.
  3. If \(B\) is the standard basis of \(V\text{,}\) then \(\underset{B'\rightarrow B}{P}\) is easy to compute. (See Remark 5.3.9.)
  4. If \(B'\) is orthogonal with respect to some inner product on \(V\text{,}\) then we can easily compute \([\boldv_j]_{B'}\) for each \(1\leq j\leq n\) using Theorem 5.1.9.
  5. If \(B\) and \(B'\) are both orthonormal bases of \(V\) with respect to a common inner product, then both change of basis matrices are orthogonal and we have \(\underset{B'\rightarrow B}{P}=(\underset{B\rightarrow B'}{P})^T\text{.}\)
