If the point \(P=(x,y)\) has polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) (so that \(x=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=r\sin\theta\)), then its image \(P'=\rho_{\alpha}(P)\) under our rotation has polar coordinates \((r,\theta+\alpha)\text{.}\) Translating back to rectangular coordinates, we see that
\begin{align*} \rho_\alpha(P)\amp= P' \\ \amp =\left(r\cos(\theta+\alpha),r\sin(\theta+\alpha)\right)\\ \amp =(r\cos\theta\cos\alpha-r\sin\theta\sin\alpha, r\sin\theta\cos\alpha+r\cos\theta\sin\alpha) \amp (\text{trig. identities}) \\ \amp=(\cos\alpha\, x-\sin\alpha\, y, \sin\alpha\, x+\cos\alpha\, y) \amp (\text{since } x=r\cos\theta, y=r\sin\theta) \text{.} \end{align*}
It follows that \(\rho_{\alpha}=T_A\text{,}\) where
\begin{equation*} A=\begin{amatrix}[rr] \cos\alpha\amp -\sin\alpha\\ \sin\alpha \amp \cos\alpha \end{amatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
as claimed.
